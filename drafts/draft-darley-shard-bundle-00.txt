       SHARD-BUNDLE and MIRROR-ATTESTATION
  Threshold Trust Primitives for the Crowsong Suite

Abstract

   This document specifies two threshold trust primitives for use
   within the Crowsong Suite [CROWSONG]:

   SHARD-BUNDLE: A structured message format for distributing
   Shamir secret-sharing shards over any channel supported by the
   the Crowsong Suite, including non-binary and human-relay channels.

   MIRROR-ATTESTATION: A multi-party content verification format
   enabling M-of-N designated mirror operators to attest that a
   mirrored content corpus matches an author's signed manifest.

   Both primitives compose existing cryptographic standards --
   Shamir secret sharing [SHAMIR] and Ed25519 [RFC8032] -- without
   introducing new cryptographic constructions.  Both primitives
   are encoded using Fox Decimal Script [FDS] for transmission
   over degraded channels.

   These primitives constitute the L4 Trust Layer of the Resilient
   Signal Stack.

1.  Introduction

1.1.  Motivation

   Secret material -- signing keys, recovery credentials, succession
   authorisations -- must often outlive the infrastructure through
   which it was originally distributed.  A signing key for a Meridian
   manifest [MERIDIAN] must remain recoverable after its custodian
   becomes unavailable, potentially in conditions where email,
   HTTPS, and IP networking are all absent.

   The answer to this problem has been known since 1979 [SHAMIR]:
   split the secret into n shards such that any k shards suffice
   for reconstruction, and distribute the shards to n trusted
   parties over independent channels.  No single party holds the
   complete secret.  Any k parties can reconstruct it.

   The gap that this document addresses is not the cryptography --
   Shamir's scheme is well-specified and well-implemented -- but
   the transport.  Existing shard distribution implementations
   assume electronic channels.  The SHARD-BUNDLE format defined
   here enables shard distribution over any channel in the
   Crowsong Suite, including printed paper and human
   courier.

1.2.  Relationship to credmgr

   The credmgr tool [CREDMGR], first published in 2012, implements
   the operational pattern formalised in this document: generate
   a secret, split it via Shamir sharing, encrypt each shard to
   the recipient's GPG public key, and distribute via email.

   SHARD-BUNDLE generalises this pattern by:

   *  Replacing GPG-encrypted email with FDS-encoded frames
      suitable for any channel (including non-electronic channels)

   *  Adding explicit contact information for shard holders in a
      channel-independent format

   *  Specifying a recovery procedure operable without the original
      software

   *  Defining a physical instantiation path for extreme-latency
      key custody

   The credmgr implementation [CREDMGR] remains a valid reference
   for the operational context of shard distribution, even where
   its specific transport (GPG/email) is not applicable.

1.3.  Design Principles

   No new cryptography.  SHARD-BUNDLE composes Shamir secret
      sharing and Ed25519.  No new cryptographic constructions
      are introduced.

   Channel independence.  A SHARD-BUNDLE MUST be transmissible
      over any channel class defined in [CROWSONG], including Class D
      (human relay) and physical media.

   Self-describing recovery.  A SHARD-BUNDLE MUST contain
      sufficient information for a recipient to understand its
      role in the threshold scheme and to initiate recovery
      without external documentation.

   Human-legible at the threshold.  The recovery procedure
      MUST be expressible in plain language within the
      SHARD-BUNDLE itself.

1.4.  Terminology

   The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL
   NOT", "SHOULD", "SHOULD NOT", "RECOMMENDED", "NOT RECOMMENDED",
   "MAY", and "OPTIONAL" are as described in BCP 14 [RFC2119]
   [RFC8174].

   Shard:  A fragment of a secret produced by Shamir (k,n)
      secret sharing, where k is the reconstruction threshold
      and n is the total number of shards.

   Shard holder:  A party designated to hold one shard and
      to participate in reconstruction when triggered.

   Secret identifier:  A human-readable label identifying the
      secret to which a shard belongs.  MUST be unique within
      a deployment.

   Reconstruction threshold (k):  The minimum number of shards
      required to reconstruct the secret.

   Total shards (n):  The total number of shards produced.

   MIRROR-ATTESTATION:  A multi-party content verification
      record signed by M of N designated mirror operators.

1.5.  Relationship to Existing Work

   Shamir Secret Sharing [SHAMIR] is the cryptographic primitive
   used for shard generation and reconstruction.

   The DNSSEC root zone key signing key management procedure
   [ICANN-DPS] uses Shamir secret sharing with physical key
   ceremonies as operational precedent for the physical key
   custody procedures specified in Section 4.

   Fox Decimal Script [FDS] is used for encoding SHARD-BUNDLEs
   for transmission over non-binary channels.

   The Meridian Protocol [MERIDIAN] is the primary consumer of
   MIRROR-ATTESTATION records.


2.  SHARD-BUNDLE

2.1.  Overview

   A SHARD-BUNDLE is a self-contained message carrying one Shamir
   shard plus the metadata necessary for:

   *  The recipient to understand what secret the shard belongs to
   *  The recipient to store the shard securely
   *  The recipient to initiate or participate in reconstruction
      when triggered
   *  The recipient to contact other shard holders

   A SHARD-BUNDLE is transmitted as the payload of an FDS-FRAME
   [FDS].  The FDS-FRAME authentication extension (Ed25519
   signature) SHOULD be used to authenticate the sender.

2.2.  Wire Format

   A SHARD-BUNDLE payload (before FDS encoding) has the following
   structure.  Fields are separated by newlines.  The separator
   line "---" separates the header fields from the shard material.

      SHARD: <index>/<total> T/<threshold>
      SECRET: <secret-identifier>
      HOLDER: <recipient-identifier>
      CREATED: <ISO8601-timestamp>
      EXPIRES: <ISO8601-timestamp or NEVER>
      CONTACTS:
      <contact-entry>
      <contact-entry>
      ...
      ---
      <shard-material>
      ---
      RECOVERY: To reconstruct <secret-identifier>, obtain shards
      from <threshold> of the <total> holders listed in CONTACTS
      above. Combine using Shamir (k,n) reconstruction with k=<k>.
      The ssss-combine tool or any conformant Shamir implementation
      may be used. Verify the reconstructed secret against its
      known hash before use.
      HASH: <hex-encoded SHA-256 of the reconstructed secret>

   Field definitions:

   SHARD:    <index>/<total> identifies this shard's position in
             the set (1-indexed).  T/<threshold> is the minimum
             number of shards required for reconstruction.

   SECRET:   Human-readable identifier for the protected secret.
             Examples: "meridian-signing-key-example.com",
             "vesper-ca-root-key-2026".  MUST be unique within
             the deployment.

   HOLDER:   Identifier for the intended recipient of this shard.
             MAY be a name, handle, or role description.

   CREATED:  ISO 8601 timestamp of shard creation.

   EXPIRES:  ISO 8601 timestamp after which this shard SHOULD be
             considered stale, or the literal string "NEVER".
             Implementors SHOULD set an expiry and rotate shards
             before expiry.

   CONTACTS: A list of contact entries for all OTHER shard holders
             (not the recipient of this specific bundle).  Format
             defined in Section 2.4.

   Shard material: The raw Shamir shard, base64url-encoded [RFC4648].

   RECOVERY: Plain-language reconstruction instructions.  MUST be
             sufficient for a technically capable person to perform
             reconstruction without additional documentation.

   HASH:     SHA-256 of the complete reconstructed secret (before
             any application-specific processing).  Used to verify
             successful reconstruction.

2.3.  Shard Material Encoding

   The Shamir shard is the output of a (k,n) threshold scheme
   applied to the secret.  The shard is base64url-encoded before
   inclusion in the SHARD-BUNDLE payload.

   Implementations SHOULD use a well-audited Shamir implementation.
   The ssss tool [SSSS] is the reference implementation used by
   credmgr [CREDMGR].  Other conformant implementations MAY be
   used provided they produce output compatible with the specified
   reconstruction procedure.

2.4.  Contact List Format

   Each contact entry in the CONTACTS section has the following
   format (one entry per line, fields separated by " | "):

      NAME: <name> | EMAIL: <address> | PHONE: <number> |
      PHYSICAL: <address> | KEY: <Ed25519 pubkey fingerprint>

   All fields are OPTIONAL.  At least one contact method MUST
   be present per entry.

   The contact list MUST include entries for all shard holders
   EXCEPT the recipient of this specific SHARD-BUNDLE.

   Rationale: In a disrupted environment, shard holders may need
   to contact each other using whatever channels remain available.
   Including physical addresses and phone numbers (not just email)
   is essential for Class D (human relay) operation.

2.5.  Physical Instantiation

   A SHARD-BUNDLE MAY be physically instantiated as a printed
   document on archival-quality paper, sealed in a durable
   container per [ARCHIVE-PROTOCOL].

   Physical SHARD-BUNDLEs MUST include:

   1.  The complete SHARD-BUNDLE content (before FDS encoding),
       printed in a human-readable font at sufficient size for
       reliable reading without magnification.

   2.  The FDS-encoded form of the SHARD-BUNDLE payload, formatted
       per [FDS] with COL/6 and WIDTH/5 defaults.

   3.  The FDS decoding procedure in plain language, sufficient
       for a person without software to decode the FDS content
       manually given a Unicode table.

   4.  Contact information for the designated recovery coordinator
       (if any), in plain text.

   Both human-readable and FDS-encoded forms MUST be present.
   The human-readable form allows direct reading without decoding.
   The FDS-encoded form enables machine processing during recovery.

2.6.  Shard Recovery Procedure

   Recovery is initiated when the trigger conditions specified in
   the associated SUCCESSION-PACKET [CROWSONG] are met, or when the
   authorised recovery coordinator determines reconstruction is
   necessary.

   Procedure:

   1.  Contact shard holders using information in the CONTACTS
       field of any available SHARD-BUNDLE.

   2.  Collect k or more shards from k or more holders.

   3.  Reconstruct the secret using a conformant Shamir
       implementation.  Reference:

          ssss-combine -t <k> -n <n>
          # Enter each shard when prompted

   4.  Verify the reconstructed secret against the HASH field.
       If verification fails, the reconstruction is invalid.
       Do not use the reconstructed value.

   5.  If physical SHARD-BUNDLEs are used, decode the FDS-encoded
       shard material per [FDS] before input to the Shamir
       implementation.


3.  MIRROR-ATTESTATION

3.1.  Overview

   MIRROR-ATTESTATION is a multi-party content verification record
   for use with the Meridian Protocol [MERIDIAN].  It enables M
   of N designated mirror operators to independently verify and
   sign the root hash of a Meridian manifest, producing a composite
   attestation that the mirrored content matches the author's
   signed manifest.

   M verified signatures constitute attestation.  Fewer than M
   verified signatures do not.

   MIRROR-ATTESTATION extends the Shamir threshold model from
   secret custody to content integrity.  The threshold structure
   is the same; the protected value is a content hash rather than
   a secret.

3.2.  Wire Format

   A MIRROR-ATTESTATION record has the following structure:

      ATTEST: <domain> v<manifest-version>
      ROOT: <hex-encoded manifest root hash>
      REQUIRED: <M> of <N>
      CREATED: <ISO8601-timestamp>
      EXPIRES: <ISO8601-timestamp>
      SIGNATURES:
      <mirror-FQDN>: <base64url Ed25519 sig over root hash>
      <mirror-FQDN>: <base64url Ed25519 sig over root hash>
      ...

   Field definitions:

   ATTEST:    Domain name and manifest version being attested.
              Example: "ATTEST: example.com v2026-03-22"

   ROOT:      The manifest root hash, computed as specified in
              [MERIDIAN] Section 3.3.

   REQUIRED:  The minimum number of valid signatures required
              to consider the attestation valid (M of N).
              M MUST be greater than 1.  M SHOULD be at least
              ceil(N/2) + 1.

   SIGNATURES: One entry per mirror operator that has verified
              the content and signed the root hash.  Additional
              entries MAY be appended as more mirror operators
              complete verification.  A MIRROR-ATTESTATION with
              fewer than M entries is pending and MUST NOT be
              used to trigger failover.

3.3.  Signature Scope

   Each signature in the SIGNATURES section is an Ed25519
   signature computed over the following input:

      UTF-8 encoding of:
      "MIRROR-ATTESTATION\n"
      + <domain> + "\n"
      + <manifest-version> + "\n"
      + <hex-encoded root hash> + "\n"

   The domain-separated prefix "MIRROR-ATTESTATION\n" prevents
   cross-protocol signature reuse.

   Verifiers MUST reconstruct this input and verify each
   signature independently.  A MIRROR-ATTESTATION is valid
   when at least M signatures verify correctly against known
   mirror operator public keys.

3.4.  Failover Integration

   When integrated with the Meridian Protocol [MERIDIAN], a
   MIRROR-ATTESTATION with M valid signatures constitutes the
   trust signal for failover activation, supplementing or
   replacing DNS-based liveness checking under conditions where
   DNS is unavailable.

   The M threshold SHOULD be specified in the Meridian manifest's
   succession terms.  In the absence of a specified threshold,
   implementations MUST require N (all signatories) rather than
   defaulting to a lower value.


4.  Key Custody Across Physical Media

4.1.  Physical Key Custody

   The L4 trust layer explicitly supports key custody via physical
   media.  A private key (or Shamir shard thereof) stored on
   archival paper, sealed in a durable container, and preserved
   under known-stable conditions is a valid key custody mechanism.

   Physical key custody is not a fallback.  For secrets that must
   survive the loss of all electronic infrastructure, physical
   key custody is the primary mechanism.

   Physical key custody SHOULD conform to [ARCHIVE-PROTOCOL] for
   material specifications.  The container, desiccant, and sealing
   requirements of [ARCHIVE-PROTOCOL] provide a baseline for
   multi-decade preservation.

4.2.  Extreme Latency Key Custody

   An extreme latency key custody arrangement -- where a shard
   is sealed in a physical archive intended to be recovered years
   or decades hence -- is a valid SHARD-BUNDLE instantiation.

   For extreme latency custody:

   *  The EXPIRES field MUST be set to a date sufficiently far in
      the future to accommodate the intended custody period, or
      to "NEVER" if no expiry is appropriate.

   *  The RECOVERY instructions MUST be written at a level of
      detail sufficient for a person with no knowledge of the
      original deployment to perform reconstruction.

   *  The FDS decoding procedure MUST be included in plain
      language within the physical archive.

   *  The Unicode table required for manual FDS decoding SHOULD
      be included (or a reference to a stable public source
      provided).

   *  Contact information SHOULD include physical addresses and
      institutional affiliations, not only email addresses.

4.3.  Recovery Documentation Requirements

   Any physical SHARD-BUNDLE intended for recovery in a disrupted
   environment MUST include the following in plain text within
   the sealed container:

   1.  What this document is and why it matters.

   2.  The reconstruction threshold and the identities of other
       shard holders.

   3.  The FDS decoding procedure (without assuming software
       availability).

   4.  The Shamir reconstruction procedure (without assuming
       the ssss tool is available -- include a description of
       the mathematical procedure or a reference to a stable
       published source).

   5.  Contact information for the recovery coordinator.

   6.  The expected use of the reconstructed secret.

   The goal is that a technically capable person who finds this
   archive in a future with no internet access and no knowledge
   of the original deployment can understand what they have found
   and what to do with it.


5.  Deployment Guidance

5.1.  Pre-Disruption Preparation

   SHARD-BUNDLEs and MIRROR-ATTESTATIONs MUST be prepared during
   normal operations, before degraded conditions occur.

   Operators MUST NOT wait for a disruption to distribute key
   material.  The value of threshold secret sharing is entirely
   in the pre-distribution.  A shard distributed after the
   disruption that made it necessary is a shard distributed
   too late.

5.2.  Shard Distribution Strategy

   Shards SHOULD be distributed over independent channels.
   Distributing all shards over the same channel (e.g., all
   via email) provides no resilience against loss of that
   channel.

   Recommended distribution strategy for a (k,n) scheme:

   *  At least one shard via electronic channel (email, secure
      messaging)

   *  At least one shard as a physical SHARD-BUNDLE (printed,
      sealed, held by a trusted party)

   *  At least one shard as an extreme-latency physical archive
      (buried or similarly preserved)

   *  If n > 3, additional shards MAY use any available channel

   This strategy ensures reconstruction is possible even if
   all electronic channels are permanently unavailable.

5.3.  Mirror Attestation Timing

   MIRROR-ATTESTATION records SHOULD be produced immediately
   after a new Meridian manifest version is published and
   mirrors have synchronised.

   Operators SHOULD not wait until failover is imminent to
   collect attestation signatures.  Collecting signatures
   under normal conditions, when all mirror operators have
   reliable connectivity, is significantly easier than
   collecting them under degraded conditions.


6.  Security Considerations

6.1.  Threshold Selection

   The reconstruction threshold k MUST be selected to balance
   availability (k small enough that reconstruction is feasible
   if some shard holders are unavailable) against security (k
   large enough that compromise of a small number of shard
   holders does not expose the secret).

   k SHOULD be at least ceil(n/2) + 1 for security-critical
   secrets.  k MAY be lower for operational convenience where
   the threat model permits.

   The MIRROR-ATTESTATION threshold M SHOULD follow the same
   guidance.

6.2.  Shard Holder Compromise

   Compromise of k-1 or fewer shard holders does not expose the
   secret.  Compromise of k or more shard holders does.

   Operators SHOULD monitor for indicators of shard holder
   compromise and rotate secrets (generating new shards and
   distributing to the same or new holder set) if compromise
   is suspected.

   Rotation requires that all existing shards be superseded.
   Operators MUST notify all shard holders when rotation occurs
   and MUST provide updated SHARD-BUNDLEs via the same
   channel diversity as the original distribution.

6.3.  Physical Media Security

   Physical SHARD-BUNDLEs are subject to physical access threats.
   Sealed containers at known locations may be discovered by
   parties other than the intended recipients.

   Physical SHARD-BUNDLEs MUST be stored at locations known only
   to the intended recipients and the recovery coordinator.

   Physical SHARD-BUNDLEs SHOULD be additionally encrypted (e.g.,
   via GPG) before printing, so that physical access to the
   container does not immediately yield the shard material.
   The decryption key may be held by the shard holder personally
   or via a separate custody arrangement.

6.4.  Timing Attacks on Attestation

   A MIRROR-ATTESTATION that has been produced but not yet
   activated (fewer than M signatures collected) MUST be treated
   as pending and MUST NOT be used for failover decisions.

   Operators MUST validate the EXPIRES field before using a
   MIRROR-ATTESTATION.  Stale attestations MUST NOT be used.

9.  Acknowledgements

   The operational pattern formalised in this document was first
   implemented in credmgr [CREDMGR], whose first commit is dated
   23 June 2012.  The jump from GPG-encrypted email to FDS-encoded
   physical archives is smaller than it appears: both are answers
   to the same question, which is how to carry a secret across a
   gap in the infrastructure to someone who needs it on the other
   side.  The lineage from that first commit to this document is
   fourteen years of the same design philosophy.

   The DNSSEC root zone key ceremony [ICANN-DPS] provides the
   most prominent existing precedent for physical key custody in
   a critical infrastructure context.  The authors of that
   procedure understood that some secrets are too important to
   trust to any single channel.
